What Was Carried, What Was Lost Chapter 28

We finally moved out of the camp after we were able to rent a small house that was next to the sea in Incheon. Even though we had a lot of money saved, we still had a difficult time finding work to sustain ourselves once we got there. Oppa was able to get a job on a fishing boat, but he told us that he would often get seasick very easily, so he would spend most of the evening sleeping. The rest of us, including Abeoji, went to the Songdo sea to catch fish, clams, and shellfish for a fishing company. But the salt from the ocean would irritate my hands and nails, my legs would ache from walking along the coast and bending down everyday. After a long day of work, I would often soak my hands in fresh warm water while Eonni cooked dinner. Though the pay was meager, we were able to survive like this for two to three years.

Later, we were finally able to buy a house for ourselves in Manseok-dong, where we moved right away. But of course, we needed to find another job in the new area.

To do this, Abeoji, Oppa, and I went to Namyang, where there were big salt fields. We worked there, but we were hungry all the time. Compared to our jobs at Incheon, we weren't paid in money, but in rice rations. This made saving up money very difficult, but we had to make do. Once we started working again, I had to cook rice everyday to pack lunch for Abeoji, and feed the rest of the family. Oftentimes, all the rice would be gone by then, so I would go hungry. It didn't bother me too much, as the others did the brunt of the work.

My hair had grown very long throughout the years of our escape, and so I wore it in two braids throughout the day—mainly to keep my hair out of my face as I carried water jars. Whenever I went out to get water, the local boys would always stare at me or follow me around.

I felt normal again. At home, even.

But the constant gnawing in my stomach and quaking in my knees when I tried to do basic chores always reminded me that I was, in fact, never going to be at home. Not soon, at least.

By then, I had learned that the body will always remember what the mind will try to let go.

For me, the sounds came to me first. The gunshots across the water. The waves when a stranger led us through the dark. The silence after Oppa disappeared, which was somehow louder than everything that came before it.

I still felt him sometimes—his weight against my back, his small delicate arms curling around my neck. I would sometimes reach for that weight and find nothing but a chill that never left my back. He had no say in any of this. Hell, he didn't even know how to talk yet. And I carried him all the way to the islands and fed him seawater soup and watched him go pale. I watched him get stripped of his future. But I told myself it would be okay. It was not okay.

She was the one who quietly took care of the whole family. She unraveled her skirt and laid it across us without a word to keep us warm. She boiled seawater and called it a meal and did not flinch while we all gagged. She crawled to a ditch because she had decided she was a burden, and when I found her, all she said was I'm sorry, Hwajae—as if her dying would have been an inconvenience to me. For all of us.

Then I saw what was beneath the blanket. And I ran.

I proved to her that the family was falling apart. That everyone had now turned to survival, and not to family.

I have spent years since trying to understand the girl who ran. I knew her reasons—the fear, the helplessness, the way the body sometimes moves before the mind can reason. I knew all of this. But it changed nothing. She had stayed for all of us, through everything. And when it was finally her turn to need someone, I dropped the blanket and fled. She died shortly after, and I was not there.

And I still ran.
