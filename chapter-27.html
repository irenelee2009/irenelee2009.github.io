What Was Carried, What Was Lost Chapter 27

Soon after Halmeoni died, Eonni and I found work at a match factory. We were paid for each tray of sticks we made by hand. It didn't give us enough pay, so we later left and found new jobs at a silk-thread mill. Compared to the match factory, this one was large and clean. But the work was exhausting. We weren't given any stools or chairs, so we had to stand all day for hours without rest, meticulously tying broken threads of silk together and tending to cocoons. My eyes strained and my fingers cramped all day, and I had headaches at night when I returned from the grueling hours of work. The earthy, musky, slightly sweet scent of the silk was pleasant, but it seemed to be everywhere even after leaving the factory. The pay was better, and we kept each other company by singing and whispering stories to each other.

Unlike us, Taejae didn't do much work. He usually stayed at the camp, playing with the older boys, and always followed them throughout the whole day. But because of that he learned a lot of bad things from them, and often ignored Eonni who was the only parental figure at the camp when Abeoji was out working far away at the school he taught at. He was twelve. Eonni was eighteen. I was fourteen.

Eonni grew increasingly upset and frustrated, often feeling stressed when Taejae disobeyed and didn't do anything to help the family other than play. Eonni would mutter angrily to me, talking about how ungrateful and lazy Taejae was while we were working at the factory. But I knew that it wasn't because he was like that.

I didn't tell Eonni that Taejae would spend most of his day watching the other kids who went to school, like me. But he didn't look at them because he wanted to study, but to have the same life as them. To have a home. Food. Clothes.

But either way, it didn't excuse his behavior towards Eonni, for when Abeoji was home, Taejae was very good and never talked back.

But one day, Taejae went too far and yelled at Eonni, storming out of the tent and out in the open where everyone turned to look at what the commotion was. He continued to yell outside, screaming at Eonni. She just sat in the tent, her hands rubbing her temples in frustration. She couldn't do anything because Taejae wouldn't listen to her. So I decided to scold him myself.

I ran out and caught him off-guard. He expected Eonni to come out and punish him, not me. His eyes widened as I pushed him down roughly onto the ground, dirt and dust flying into the air as I kept him down with my arms. He coughed from the dirt and yelled in pain. I then flipped him onto his stomach, his face in the dirt, and I sat on his back, my weight making his tiny body gasp. His voice seemed to rise a pitch higher as he squealed with discomfort.

Everyone gathered around and watched me with wide eyes, but deep down they all knew why I was treating my younger brother so roughly. They knew how he treated Eonni, so nobody stepped in to intervene.

Gritting my teeth, I sat rooted on his back as he tried squirming to escape, but I didn't budge.

"If you don't listen to your sister, I'll punish you just like this in front of everyone. I can sit here all day, Taejae, while your friends watch you getting humiliated like this. You must respect her," I said loudly, making sure that Taejae heard every word to the bone.

I wanted to teach him discipline, since Halmeoni was no longer with us, and Abeoji didn't have time to teach him anymore. Taejae stayed stubbornly quiet, but I lightly slapped the back of his head and he immediately blurted, "Fine!".

I stayed on his back for a few more minutes before I returned to our tent—leaving him lying on the dirt.
