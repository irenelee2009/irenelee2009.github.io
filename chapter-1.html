What Was Carried, What Was Lost Chapter 1

 My father and brother had fled. South Korean troops storming towards our lands had ordered every able man into the North Korean army, no matter the circumstances.

But if you were sent to the army, you would die. That was the end of the story. There were no exceptions, no mercy, no second chances. This was war.

Sitting on the cold wooden floor of my living room, I cradled my one-year-old brother—Yeomjae—in my arms. Since Abeoji and Oppa were gone, Eonni was in the fields outside, tending to the rice paddies and garden. I heard the rhythmic thwack of the hoe as she slammed it onto the ground to dig out the weeds. Taejae, my younger brother of ten, lay beside me, staring up at the ceiling while reciting his arithmetic.

Abeoji — Father; a respectful term of address used within the family.
Oppa — Older brother; used by females to address or refer to an older male sibling.
Eonni — Older sister; used by females to address or refer to an older female sibling.

I stared at our bookshelf, admiring the books that filled each level. On the highest level, however, was a straw doll. Eomeoni had made it for Eonni when she was younger. I let out a heavy sigh as I remembered the faint memories of Eomeoni. She died ten days after giving birth to the child that was now sleeping in my arms. Our home felt hollow with the echoes of Eomeoni’s witty jokes lingering faintly in its corners, nearly forgotten.

Eomeoni — Mother; a respectful term of address used within the family.

Soon after Eomeoni passed, Halmeoni took over the household chores, doing as much as her body could allow.

Halmeoni — Grandmother; a term of endearment and respect for one's grandmother.

My responsibility was Yeomjae. After school, I always carried him on my back, my nose buried in my schoolbook as I walked from house to house. Women would feed him because he was too young for normal food that we ate.

Compared to the other civilians of Hwanghae province, we were lucky. Wealthier. Our home had five rooms and an outhouse; our fields kept us well fed. We owned cattle, though they were used for the millstone, not for eating.

I gently laid Yeomjae on his blankets and listened to Halmeoni’s loud shuffling as she slept in the next room. After taking over most of the household chores, she needed an afternoon nap just to stay upright.

 “I’m done!” Taejae sat up suddenly, beaming with pride, “Did you hear me? I finished reciting everything perfectly.”

 “Great,” I said dully, staring out the window. I glanced at Taejae and saw him glaring at me, frowning.

 “Did you even listen to me?”

 “Of course I did.” I lied. My arms ached from holding the baby. My throat ached even more.

I stood up to go fetch water from the backyard well, gliding across the smooth floorboards. Just as I reached for the backdoor—

Thunk

I froze. The sound came from the other side. Through the front window, I could still see Eonni bent over the paddies. Taejae immediately stood up upon hearing the noise as well and stared at me with wide eyes. No one should be here. Maybe an animal?

 “Hwajae,” a deep yet familiar voice whispered. Cold ice ran down my spine. No. He shouldn’t be here, “It’s me. Let me in, quick!”

My hands fumbled for the latch. The door flew open, and my older brother stumbled in, slamming it behind him. He collapsed onto the floor, his back against the door. Taejae’s mouth was ajar.

 “Oppa! What are you doing here? You’re supposed to be with Abeoji, heading to the islands.” I blurted, forgetting that Yeomjae was still asleep. He suddenly awoke with a start and started to cry. Oppa sighed with fatigue. Taejae rushed to the baby and rocked him to silence, and eventually back to sleep.

 “The inmingun almost caught us,” he said. “Abeoji told me to run. I ran and hid for days.”

inmingun — The Korean People's Army; the military force of North Korea.

 I tried not to frown in confusion, but he saw it anyway.

 “He told me it was important to split up. I didn’t know where else to go.” His eyes flashed with…panic?

My mouth became dry, and I stared at Oppa, not knowing how to react. He brought danger to our door. He left Abeoji alone.

“But–” I stopped when Taejae tugged my sleeve. I looked down to see Taejae giving me a subtle shake of his head. Right. He was the eldest son. After Abeoji, his word was law.

Oppa closed his eyes and sighed, tipping his head back against the door. “You don’t know what hell we’ve been through. Living off bark and rats, and you don’t even welcome me back.”

Hell? My lips pressed together. What did he know of hell—leaving us behind to save himself, while we waited for soldiers to drag us away? But I swallowed the thought.

“When The Leader condemns us, only then will we know hell.” I said quietly, reining in my disbelief.

He grumbled in quiet agreement and looked away, trudging to his room and collapsing on his bed without even bothering to close the door.

Night fell. Eonni and Halmeoni were in a room with Oppa, whispering. I pressed my ear to the wooden door but could barely make out their words. I shifted lower, my ear to the gap between the door and the floor. My eyes lit up when I finally heard their voices.

“This can’t be,” Eonni whispered. “Are they looking for you?”

“Yes. No doubt. Abeoji is still on the islands. I snuck back to the mainland for food, but they found me and threw me into the army camp. But I managed to escape.”

“What are we going to do? We don’t have time, and we certainly are in no state of defecting. We have Taejae, Yeomjae, and Hwajae. Even Jungjae is just sixteen. They need food, school–”

“I know, Halmeoni.” Oppa whispered, his voice low but urgent, “But I can’t leave now. If the inmingun comes, the villagers will report us. I’ll hide in the storage room.”

“Then we must prepare to die,” Halmeoni said, her voice hardening. “By coming back here, you might have just sealed our fate.”

Silence.

“I won’t let that happen. I swear it.”

A cool breeze rushed through the gap and into my ear. They were standing up. I quickly shuffled backwards and across the living room as they opened the door. I pretended to look busy by flipping through my school textbooks, but my thoughts seemed to swirl in my head. The army? So he lied. He said that Abeoji told him to run, but he was actually caught stealing food. So where was Abeoji? Was he still on the islands, somewhere safe? Or was he starving right now with no food, near death while we ate good food? I shuddered at that thought.

 “Hwajae, come here.” Oppa’s voice stretched across the room. I froze and looked up at his somber dark eyes. I nodded, and walked to him. He was quite tall, and always had a friendly demeanor surrounding him, that is, until all of this happened. “I assume that your school has taught you who the inmingun are, right?” The army. The soldiers.

I nodded again, the words not forming in my mouth.

 “They are after me.” Oppa announced. Taejae, who was behind him, froze. “It is because they consider me a defector to the nation. Do you know what a defector is?”

I recoiled slightly, but nodded again, the words barely escaping my lips. “They are people who betray our nation. People who are evil and whose minds are bad. But you weren’t—”

 “No. That is what they teach you, but that is not the real reason why we defect.” Oppa said, “We defect because there are more opportunities out there, Hwajae. Better opportunities. You can dream bigger, and get whatever you want. But now, we defected because we wanted to survive, and not join the army.”

 I furrowed my brow and took a step back in confusion. Better opportunities? As if. The Leader told us that the world outside was poverty-stricken, filled with horrible violent people who looked strange. But Oppa snatched my arm and kept me from moving away. “I’m telling the truth. If the inmingun, or even anyone asks you anything about this, say that you don’t know. If not, we will all be in danger.”

I glanced behind Oppa and saw Eonni, Taejae, and Halmeoni looking at me. “What are we going to do then, if the inmingun knows about this already?”

 “I don’t know.” Oppa sighed and let go of my arm. “But I will hide in the storage room for now.”

 He then left the living room and headed towards the storage room. The storage room was small and hidden away in another large room that was connected to the living room. Its walls were lined from floor to ceiling with food and our harvested goods, with barely any room to squeeze past to the back. There, Oppa would stay until we had a plan.

✦

 It had been two weeks since Oppa went into hiding. Nothing had changed. The inmingun hadn’t come either. The tension finally settled deeply into every crevice and niche of our home, blanketing us into an eerie silence. We couldn’t do anything but wait and plan.

 “Why can’t we let him out now? The inmingun hadn’t come yet, and it has already been two weeks.” Taejae grumbled at the dinner table, stuffing his mouth full of rice.

 “You know that’s dangerous, Taejae.” Halmeoni said, cringing at the pieces of rice falling from his chin. Taejae just grumbled some more, but didn't say anything else.

 “Do we have a plan?” I said as I turned to Eonni, the candle behind her flickering. “What about the inmingun? You went out a couple of times this week to tend to the fields. What did you see? Did anyone approach you?” I still had a million questions but I snapped my mouth shut when I saw Eonni shake her head with exhaustion and fear.

 “I don’t know.” She said as she dropped her spoon and stared at it on the dinner table, “I really don’t know.”
