What Was Carried, What Was Lost Chapter 9

 The village was crowded with people, its narrow streets packed with merchants. Objects and food were laid out on top of large pieces of cloth on the ground, some with a small canopy above their heads to protect themselves from the sun.

Eonni gave me fifty won, which I had tightly in my hand. My other hand was firmly locked with Eonni’s. She didn’t want me to get lost, especially in crowded streets like this.

“Bread for ten won! Freshly baked bread for ten won!” An old woman’s voice cried out. Looking over my shoulder, I examined the bread that was on top of the woman’s cloth. They looked small, and most definitely not freshly baked. Looking over a few more stalls, a different one had bigger and fresher bread for ten won.

 “Should we buy these? Five for fifty won is perfect. One for all of us.” I whispered to Eonni without the seller noticing. If they thought you were interested in their goods, they would never let you go.

 “Perhaps.” She nodded and beckoned me to buy five. Walking towards the stall, the seller noticed me and immediately waved to come closer. She was an old woman with white hair and dark wrinkly skin from life-long sun exposure. Her teeth were yellow, but her fingers were clean and not stained from dirt.

 “Five for fifty won?” I asked as I inspected the loaves that were piled on top of a large wooden plate. They looked fresh, the warm scent of newly baked bread wafting up to my nose. My stomach grumbled.

 “Yes, yes! I’ll gather them up for you.” The woman eagerly stored five large loaves into a wrapped cloth bundle. “You’re lucky, girl. This is my first batch that I made just now. Usually my customers would flood my stall, but you came before all of them.” She gave me a large yellow smile.

 I smiled awkwardly and accepted the bundle with two hands and a small bow. It was important to always show respect to elders. Taking out the small folded paper of fifty won, I flinched when she yanked it out of my hand.

 “Sorry for that, girl! You know people here steal the second they see won in the air. It disappears like poof!” She pocketed the bill and made a fake explosion with her hands.

I chuckled nervously and thanked her again. Briskly walking back, I saw Eonni with crossed arms, staring at me with disapproval.

 “Why did you strike up a conversation with her? We have to keep a low profile.” She frowned as she whispered into my ear.

 “It’s fine,” I rolled my eyes as I handed her the bundle of bread, “She was the one who talked, I barely said anything. If we look scared, they’ll notice.”

 Eonni sighed but gave me a sharp glance. “Do you think this will be enough? We still have two hundred won from Halmeoni’s savings.”

 “Maybe something more filling?” I suggested, “I’m starving.”

 “Yes, let's.” Eonni nodded and we both set off deeper into the market.

 The sun was falling over the horizon when we made our way back to Halmeoni and the others, painting the sky with streaks of orange and gold. The market had died down into a quiet buzz as sellers closed up for the night. My arms were full of goods, as were Eonni’s. Tonight would be a feast.

We had spent one hundred won after buying the bread, and with that we were able to buy many things. My mouth watered as I smelled the sweet and savory aromas of the food, and Eonni was complaining about starving the whole way up.

 Taejae was the first to see us arrive, and ran to see what we bought. The sky was now turning a dark hue, and the crickets started their scratchy tune.

 “Sikhye!” He squealed when he took a bundle out of my arms. Inside were two small cans of cold sikhye, a traditional rice drink that was sweet and refreshing. He snatched another bundle, the smallest one, and would have screamed with delight had Halmeoni not shushed him. It was a rare sight: a small bag of colorful hard candy.

Sikhye — A traditional Korean sweet rice drink, served cold and often enjoyed as a refreshing treat.

“How did you get candy and sikhye? You didn’t spend all the money, did you?” Halmeoni raised her eyebrows as she beheld the goods. She had Yeomjae on her back, and did not have hands to comb through the packages as well.

“No, Halmeoni. We still have one hundred won.” Eonni handed her the bills as Taejae rummaged through the rest of the goods. I took Yeomjae from Halmeoni and placed him on my lap as I sat down on the grass. Everyone followed and we sat in a circle, with all the food inside it.

Bread, sikhye, candy, and one large roasted mackerel sat in the center of us all, its rich scents making us all drool. It felt like forever since we had a proper meal together. My fingers twitched to grab a loaf of bread, but I held back as Halmeoni reached for one herself. The eldest always took the first bite before everyone dug in. Usually, I had waited patiently with much respect, but now it felt like eternity as I watched her bring the bread to her mouth and take a large bite. As soon as she took a bite, I lunged for my loaf, nearly stuffing my face with it as everyone else reached for theirs. Well, everyone except Yeomjae.

“Oh no! I totally forgot to buy milk.” Eonni gasped with her bread still midair in her grasp.

We all froze as we watched Yeomjae gurgle angrily with hunger, his small fists waving furiously in the air.

“Perhaps we can feed him sikhye?” Taejae suggested.

“Too sweet.” Halmeoni shook her head. “Dip the bread in the water that we have so that it is soft enough to eat.”

“I’m sorry,” Eonni’s shoulders sagged, “I should have thought about Yeomjae.”

“It’s not your fault,” Halmeoni smiled, “Besides, milk is rarer than candy these days. You wouldn’t have found it anyway.”

Silence unfolded again as we all watched Yeomjae nibble the soaked pieces of bread from Taejae’s fingers. Fortunately, he didn’t have any issues and seemed to enjoy the change.

The sun had completely settled by then, and the humming of animals began to fill the air, their tunes being the only sound in the stillness other than the rise and fall of our breaths. Halmeoni had gone to bed early and I couldn’t blame her for that. When her snores echoed in the darkness, we decided to go to bed ourselves.

Settling beside Halmeoni, I let out a breath of exhaustion as I stared at the starry sky above me. The tree tops covered most of it, but I was still able to see the twinkling lights from the stars above. With Taejae and Yeomjae to my left and Eonni next to them, I couldn’t fall asleep as they did. Everything felt too still. It was pleasant, but disturbing. How come nobody had tried to come look for us? Someone from the village, perhaps? They must have noticed by now that our home was completely dark, our fields growing weeds. My eyelids felt sluggish and heavy, but sleep did not come. It was as if my senses had become sharper and more aware of every little noise of the forest. The creak of the trees, the shuffles of the leaves, the grass shifting next to my ears.

Soon, the peace would be disturbed. Our time of leisure hidden in the mountains would be gone. Tomorrow, we would go down to the coast one last time, and never return to the mountains again.
