What Was Carried, What Was Lost Chapter 26

Our space at the refugee camp consisted of two large straw mats on the concrete floor in a large building.

When we first arrived, I missed taking care of the eight-year-old kid at the barn. There was no company there, other than my own family. Not that I didn't like our conversations, it just started getting exhausting. We spent longer than a year there, enduring sweltering summers and biting winters in the big concrete building that did nothing against the seasons that blurred into a single, grinding struggle. At first, food was ladled out in bowls; later, rations of rice were distributed and families cooked outdoors in large cauldrons over wood fires. There was always a fight outside the building—some with kids, and others with grown adults fighting over food. It was never quiet at the camp, except when everyone was at work during the day. The camp housed people from all over North Korea such as Hamgyeongbuk-do, Pyongyang, and many other regions.

The camp wasn't necessarily nice, though it was much better than where we could have ended up. However, there were memories of the camp that had been engraved into my mind. I remember when there was a pregnant woman giving birth in the corner of the building. With only mats hung up for her meager privacy, she labored while demanding her husband to light her a cigarette—smoking through the pain as she delivered her child. The building soon turned hazy and gray as she smoked and smoked nonstop. I had to stay outside the building the whole day. Her screams echoed in the camp, jarring my ears, a testament to a life entering the world with no welcome. No medical assistance was given to her, either.

The scene was unforgettable, and I couldn't sleep for days.

Across the main road from the camp stood Chonbuk National University and a middle school. I often watched students in their neat, colorful uniforms walk to class with backpacks and a purpose. I\'d stand there, behind the fence that separated the civilians with the refugees, lingering until the last student had vanished behind the school gates, a knot of sorrow and envy in my stomach.

However, I was still able to earn money by selling gum at the train station. At first, I noticed a few children doing it, carrying makeshift trays and running to the train as it stopped in front of the station. Determined to do the same, I bought gum from some of the children at the station with the little money we had, and found a wooden board that I used as a tray.

With the tray in my hand, I would run to the train while yelling, "Gum! Gum for sale!" as the passengers streamed out from the train. The passengers all looked wealthy, all wearing clean warm coats with fancy hats. I always wondered what it was like to be on a train.

Many children also sold gum at the same time, but I was quicker. When a passenger raised their hand to signal that they wanted gum, I would sprint to the person as fast as I could, always outrunning most of the others. I was on the smaller side than most of the children, but that allowed me to weave through the crowds of tall people, my feet quick and light.

I was able to earn well, and soon I stopped buying gum from the children to resell when I found out where they were buying all their supplies from—an American Market some distance away.

One day, I went there myself and was startled by the amount of people that swarmed the market. It was totally different from the markets that I had seen in North Korea. Unlike the small stalls I knew from North Korea, this market had multiple open tents with tables full of foreign items underneath them. There were soldiers walking around too, but not Korean ones—American soldiers. They seemed to enjoy the market's business, and didn't look like they were patrolling. Instead, they smoked and laughed together. No guns were in sight too.

At first, I couldn't navigate through the crowds of people, but eventually found some gum and candy and bought it with the money that I earned. I was also able to buy food like buns and noodles for my family.

Halmeoni also tried to earn money for the family by pulling heavy ropes at a port as work. She was one of the thinnest and weakest out of all of us, but she was determined to contribute to the family to make our lives a little less miserable. She never complained about how hard the work was, because it was the only one they offered. But one day a rope snapped and flung her backwards, breaking her leg. Crippled and in constant pain, she stopped working and could only sit or lie on our mats.

One afternoon, as I returned from watching the students, filled with my usual ache of longing, Halmeoni was gone. Panic seized me. I called her name, searching frantically. The camp was nearly empty; that day, everyone was at work including the rest of my family, except for me. Then, a neighbor ran toward me, his face wild with alarm.

He shouted, waving his arms to signal me to follow him.

I ran after him, my legs moving as fast as they could, my heart pounding. Did something happen? The neighbor brought me to a place outside of camp, and I found her in a deep ditch. She had crawled there on her hands, intending to end her life. The kind neighbor helped me carry her back to safety, hot tears streaming down my face as I saw the depth of her hopelessness. "I'm sorry, Hwajae," was all she said over and over again.

Eventually she was moved to a separate makeshift shelter—little more than a roof with open sides—because she could no longer move on her own.

Halmeoni would always cry with joy whenever I brought her food that I bought with the money I earned, saying, "My Hwajae even brings me food," as she wept. She never had company during the day as we all went to work. She was alone and spent her days staring at the heat hazes in the air.

One sweltering summer day, she called to me weakly and asked me to lift her blanket. I did, and recoiled in horror. Maggots swarmed on the mat beneath her, feeding on her waste.

Terrified, I dropped the blanket and fled, the image of that horror seared into my mind. I regret it to this day—the fear that made me abandon her, the chance I lost to offer her one last act of love and comfort. She died shortly after.

When Abeoji returned from his teaching job, he buried her on a small plot of land on a mountain, offered by a kind stranger.

Soon after, Oppa returned after being part of the army, only to find Halmeoni gone. Forever. He wept, a deep, bitter sound. He lost both Yeomjae and Halmeoni without knowing, and could not say his goodbyes again. It was the first time I\'d seen him cry for a very long time.

I regretted everything I didn't do for her.
