What Was Carried, What Was Lost Chapter 7

 Rain attacked us like bullets as it struck our bodies. It fell down so fast that it looked like there was mist suspended in the air, clouding our vision. My worn shoes made awful squelching noises as I trekked across the muddy paths and past the flooded rice paddies, my toes numb from the cold. Taejae walked beside me, with Halmeoni and Eonni in front. We didn’t utter a single word.

My clothes were soaked through with rain.

The village seemed to stretch on forever. The soldiers who patrolled the villages were either inside or asleep, since the rain was so severe. Thunder cracked overhead, again and again.

My thighs were burning by the time we made it to the end of the village. Piles of mountains stretched far in front of us now. Yeomjae, who was on my back, was fortunately silent but added an extra weight that I needed to carry. I felt his small breaths as he squirmed in the cloth wrap, as if the steady pelts of rain had lulled him to sleep.

The dewy smell of wet grass filled my nose as we journeyed across the wet fields.

I didn’t know where I was going, but my feet aimlessly followed the steps of Eonni and Halmeoni. The only thoughts that ran through my head were the number of steps I took since we left our house. Soon, our path started to incline, which meant that we were on a mountain. Taejae had followed silently next to me, not saying a single word since we left. I spared a glance at him and saw his eyes fixed on the ground.

“How much longer?” Eonni asked Halmeoni, her voice muffled by the rain.

 “We just started, Jungjae. Walking to the coast will take a few more hours, but that's without any rest and keeping a consistent pace.”

 “But it felt like three already! Surely the coast is not so far away from us now. I could always smell the saltiness of the ocean from our rice paddy field back at home.”

Home.

There, it was my life.

Who was I now?

 I was still Shin Hwajae, a thirteen-year-old-girl who lives in Hwanghae province with her family. No. Lived. I was not that girl anymore. To this nation, I was now a traitor.

My whole body was cramping as I made one final effort before we stopped underneath large trees that shielded most of the rain. Once the steady fall of rain diminished, Yeomjae awoke from my back. Halmeoni sighed in relief as she took a seat on one of the tree’s exposed roots, wringing her clothes of water. We all did the same when we claimed our own spots. I looked behind me and saw our town that was now touching the end of the horizon, so far down below me. Being up on a mountain had its perks, but I still longed to be in our comfortable home again.

 “We must rest here for the next day. We’ll travel at first light.” Halmeoni spoke after a few moments of silence.

 “But wouldn’t we be spotted?” Taejae asked.

 “Not here. Nobody would venture this far. Besides, everyone will be occupied in caring for their flooded fields from all this rain.” Eonni shook her head.

 “To get to the coast, there will be one more mountain we must cross. But first, we must sleep. Jungjae, take the first watch.” Halmeoni didn’t even bother to look at her for an answer before she plopped against the tree’s trunk and fell into an exhausted sleep. The rain’s violent assault was muffled in the forest, with only a few streaks of rain poking through the thick tree tops. I settled against a partially dry tree after unwrapping Yeomjae from my back. I held his frail body against my chest and stared at two ants that crawled onto my muddy shoes. I didn’t even bother to flick them off.

When everyone was asleep, I looked up and watched Eonni as she started to hum a quiet tune, her back facing me. I recognized the song right away. It was The Leader’s Anthem that we had always sung in the beginning of our classes. We would sing it with confidence, pride, and energy while we stood by our desks, facing the portrait of The Leader. But Eonni’s voice was drowned with what sounded like betrayal as she sang, her voice quiet and fragile. Memories started to spill into my mind, the days of innocence and bliss, the moments of joy and happiness—when I sat under our fruit trees and read my books, when I ran around the village, greeting our neighbors and entering their homes for a sweet treat. I missed it all.

 “Go to sleep, Hwajae,” Eonni whispered, not even bothering to turn around.

 “How did you know I was awake?” I furrowed my brows, but my eyelids started to get heavy.

 “You started singing along,” Eonni said.

 Oh. Unknowingly, I had started to hum with her as I replayed my memories.

 “Well then, goodnight.” I said, my voice falling into a faint whisper.

Before I could hear Eonni’s reply, darkness consumed me.
