What Was Carried, What Was Lost Chapter 23

He slept for two days straight, and we almost thought that he really was dead. But his loud snores reassured us that he was indeed still alive.

Carrying him to our tent was disturbingly easy, as his body was as light as a feather. He looked like a feather too: his skin a pale gray, his eye sockets sunken into his skull, and his limbs freakishly thin. He was thinner than Eonni. He was just like the villagers had described him: skin and bones with bloodshot eyes, but worse.

He didn’t have a shirt anymore—just pants that were thin and filled with tears. After putting him down on dry grass, we lit the fire to give him warmth. While we waited, we also didn’t eat the loaf of bread that we had bought when we were walking back from the market—saving it for Oppa. Taejae, Eonni, and I went to the market during those two days to sell firewood while Halmeoni and Abeoji watched Oppa. We were able to buy two more loaves of bread.

When he woke up, he stared at us silently, his eyes wide and unmoving. We all stared back with unease, with my finger anxiously tapping my knee. He couldn’t sit up on his own, so Abeoji gently propped him against a tree. Halmeoni had already given him a little water, which he gratefully drank. Now, silence blanketed us as we waited for either Oppa or Abeoji to speak.

“You all look well.” He finally said, his voice sounding like sawdust.

“You’ve certainly looked better,” Abeoji replied. “How are you feeling?”

I suppressed myself from closing my eyes in disbelief. How could Abeoji’s first words to him be: you’ve certainly looked better?

However, Oppa just laughed and shook his head with a smile. We showed him the loaf of bread we saved for him, and his eyes immediately lit up. It was dangerous to give it all to him whole, so instead, we gave him small chunks soaked in water for him to swallow slowly.

When he finally finished the loaf of bread and sighed with relief, he then looked at his left arm that was wrapped with a piece of cloth, which we realized was his shirt. It was stained red, and I nearly gasped when I recognized it as a wound.

“I was hit by a bullet on the boat, and I fell overboard by the impact,” He began after noticing my stares, his voice stronger than moments before, “The bullet just grazed my arm—so no bones were broken—but it went pretty deep. Don’t worry, the wound is basically healed.”

“What happened after that?” Taejae blurted. “You know, when we lost you on the boat.” I silently thanked Taejae’s eleven-year-old naivety for making him ask the difficult questions for us.

“When I fell overboard, the current swept me under the boat, and I think I hit my head against the wood pretty hard because it knocked me out. I woke up on the shore—I think all the soldiers had left by then. I was really lucky, but I think that head injury did something because I couldn’t move my legs for weeks.” Oppa shook his head in shock, as if he was remembering the moment. I watched in horror as he reached for his legs and kneaded them tenderly, as if making sure that he didn’t lose feeling in them again.

“I’m assuming that they are better now?” Abeoji asked. Oppa nodded and continued his story.

“Some soldiers came back to collect the dead bodies that wound up back on shore. Thankfully, they didn’t do a thorough search, because if they had, they would have found me crawling farther into the forest to hide.

When I finally regained my senses, I had to find a way to get to the islands, since I knew you guys were going there. I didn’t expect you to come back for me, since the boat was so badly damaged and the soldiers would have seen it if you did. I first needed food, so it took me a long time to concoct something digestible with the things I foraged and regain some of my strength.

At first, I tried to build a raft, but I didn’t have the proper materials. So after a few failed attempts, I gave up. I barely got any sleep because I was afraid that the inmingun would find me and capture me in my sleep, so I just walked up the coast the whole time. The most I slept was less than an hour every day. I didn’t know how much time had passed…weeks? Months? I kept walking all that time.”

Sleepless nights. The most I’d ever gone without sleeping this whole time was one night. I couldn’t imagine how that would feel on top of a horrifying head injury, no food or water, and being completely alone. I took a deep breath, steadying myself as Oppa continued.

“But then one lucky night, I saw a boat coming to shore from the direction of the islands. It was a small boat—really narrow—and had no sails. Only two men were on it.”

“A narrow boat?” Halmeoni said with furrowed brows, “Were they from Seommaeup?”

“Yes, they were,” Oppa nodded, “They said they came here to search for any food that they could get as a ‘last try’—I didn’t know what that meant—but they allowed me to come to their island with them after I helped them find some food. That’s how I arrived.”

“I’m relieved that they were able to make another boat and find some food,” Eonni sighed, “They were in a pretty rough shape when we left—they didn’t have food for months.”

“Yeah, it looked like it when I got there. Everyone looked very thin and weak,” Oppa replied, “But when I got there, I didn’t see you guys, so I thought that you had already moved on since there wasn't any food left there.”

“But how did you get to the other islands if you didn’t have a boat yourself?” I asked.

Oppa ran his fingers through his sandy hair, “To go to the second island, Seommaeup allowed me to use theirs, as long as one of them was on it with me to bring it back. To go from the second to the third, however… I had to swim.”

“Swim?” Abeoji gasped, “But it’s such a long way! How did you manage?”

“Well, it was difficult, but it was possible,” Oppa shrugged, “I got tossed and turned here and there, and when I was tired, I just floated on my back. It took two days to get there, but I had a plank of wood to use as a raft.”

“Just to make sure, you’re not crazed, right? Because I would hate to be near you if you were.” Eonni grinned playfully at Oppa, who laughed in response. His laughter felt like sparks in my ears, jarring the air.

“No,” Oppa shook his head while smiling, “I don’t know how that rumor went around, but it led you to me, so I have no complaints.”

“What happened when you got to this island?” I asked. I didn’t understand how Eonni could joke around. Glaring at her, she just smiled. I sighed and returned my attention to Oppa.

“Well, I knew that you had to be on this island, or you would have already gone to South Korea. But it was so big, and I was so exhausted from the hours of swimming, that I completely collapsed for a day on the beach. A kind man gave me something to eat and drink, so I felt a bit replenished after that, but walking was still painfully sore,” Oppa replied as he started massaging his thighs, “I walked all around the island thrice to see if you guys were around, but I realized after that you were probably inland. But I didn’t have the energy to go up the hills yet. Walking along the coast was the only thing I could do, and once I got on this island, I didn’t stop.”

My mind flashed to when Oppa didn’t stop walking even when he was chest-deep in the ocean. My throat constricted, and I sat in reeling silence as I took in the man who talked so casually before me.

“And then we came,” Abeoji said.

“Yes,” Oppa nodded with a smile, “You came, and I felt so relieved that I completely passed out. To be honest, I was scared that you guys had left me and had already gone to South Korea. I felt like giving up—but I wanted to at least die trying, so I decided to try to swim to South Korea, no matter how impossible it was. But that was when you guys found me, so I was really relieved.”

We all sat in silence, taking everything in.

“Don’t worry so much, I’m fine, really.” Oppa said, “After a few more days of rest, I think I’ll be perfectly fine.”

“That’s good to hear. We’re sorry we couldn’t do much for you.” Halmeoni said, “It’s great to see you again.”

Oppa smiled. He sighed with contentment and then looked around as if looking for something. “Anyways, where’s Yeomjae?”
